The Outsider CEO Playbook

Prepared for Philippe Beaulieu · February 21, 2026

I have been thinking about your situation at Mecademic, and I want to share something I have seen go wrong in companies that look a lot like yours right now.

You were promoted to CEO four months ago. The founders are still on the leadership team. You just announced a $21M raise. And the Mecademic website still lists Jonathan Coulombe as CEO.

That last detail might seem like a small thing. It is not. It is a signal of the transition that has not fully happened yet, and in my experience, these signals compound.

Harvard Business Review published a study in January showing that founder-to-new-CEO transitions fail at two to three times the rate of other CEO changes. The reason is almost never that the people involved are bad at their jobs. The reason is ambiguity. Nobody knows who is really in charge. The founder keeps making calls in their old domain. The new CEO has the title but not the full room. And the longer that ambiguity runs, the harder it is to resolve, because people build habits around it.

You are at month four. The window for setting the tone is still open, but it is closing.

Here is what I have seen work. It comes down to one thing: the new CEO has to narrate the transition out loud, before anyone else narrates it for them.

The sentence that does the most work is something like: “Jonathan and Ilian built the world’s smallest, most precise industrial robot. My job is to make sure the world gets to use it.” That framing is specific to Mecademic but the structure is universal. It credits the founders without deferring to them. It claims the future without erasing the past. And it gives everyone in the company a clean story about who does what.

The people you are about to hire in the US, Europe, and Asia will never have met the founders. They will not know the origin story unless you tell it. And the version you tell will become the company’s operating narrative whether you intend it to or not.

Skygauge Robotics, another Canadian robotics company, went through this exact dynamic two months ago. They brought in a new CEO and moved the co-founder to Chief Product Officer. The public framing was “planned succession that allows the founder to focus on what he does best.” That language is worth studying. It turns a power shift into a compliment.

If I were advising you, I would say: write three paragraphs this week. Where Mecademic came from. What changed. Where it is going. Show it to the founders before you show it to anyone else. Not for approval. For fingerprints. Then use it in every all-hands and every onboarding for the next six months.

The CEO who writes the story first is the one who leads. The one who waits discovers, a year later, that three competing versions have taken root.
