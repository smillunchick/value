The Outsider
CEO Playbook

How to establish authority when the founders are still in the building — and the website still lists someone else as CEO.

Prepared for Philippe Beaulieu · February 2026

Where you are right now

Four months into the CEO role,
with four founders still on the team.

Mecademic just announced a $21M raise, which is a strong signal of confidence from the board. But there's a smaller signal that matters just as much: the company website still lists Jonathan Coulombe as CEO.

That's not a website bug. It's a symptom of a transition that hasn't fully landed yet — and in these situations, the small signals are the ones that compound.

higher failure rate for founder-to-new-CEO transitions compared to other leadership handovers

The research is clear that the cause is almost never incompetence. It's ambiguity — nobody is quite sure who's actually making the calls, and the longer that ambiguity runs, the harder it is to unwind.

The pattern to watch for

The risk isn't conflict between
you and the founders. It's drift.

What typically happens

The founder keeps making decisions in their original domain — not out of ego, but out of habit. The new CEO holds the title but not the full room. Over time, people stop asking who decides and just route around the question entirely.

Why it gets worse with time

Every week without explicit clarity, the ambiguity hardens into culture. New hires learn the informal power structure. By month eight or nine, what was a soft dynamic becomes an entrenched one that requires real political capital to change.

Move 1: Name it before anyone else does

Credit the past.
Claim the future.

"Jonathan and Ilian built the world's smallest, most precise industrial robot. My job is to make sure the world gets to use it."

A sentence like that does a surprising amount of work. It acknowledges the founders' contribution without deferring to them. It claims the commercial mandate without diminishing the technical one. And it gives everyone in the company a clean, repeatable story about who does what.

Move 2: Make the lanes explicit

Say who owns what — out loud,
in writing, and more than once.

Founders own

Engineering, product, and R&D — the precision that defines the Mecademic brand. This is where their expertise is irreplaceable, and where the company needs them most.

CEO owns

Growth, global expansion, governance, and commercial strategy — the work of turning a breakthrough product into a worldwide business. This is the mandate the board hired you for.

Skygauge Robotics, another Canadian robotics company, went through this exact transition in December 2025. Their public framing was "planned succession that allows the founder to focus on what he does best." That language turns a power shift into a compliment — worth studying.

Move 3: Write the origin story before it writes itself

The next 100 hires will never
meet the founders in person.

The people joining Mecademic in the US, Europe, and Asia-Pacific will know the company through your version of the story. That version becomes the operating narrative whether you write it deliberately or not — so you should write it deliberately.

Three paragraphs: where Mecademic came from, what changed, and where it's going. Show it to the founders first — not for approval, but for fingerprints. Then use it in every all-hands and every onboarding for the next six months.

The CEO who writes the story first is the one who leads. The one who waits discovers, a year later, that three competing versions have taken root.
