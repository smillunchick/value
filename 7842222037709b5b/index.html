CEO Playbook

A framework for establishing authority
when the founders are still in the building

Prepared for Philippe Beaulieu · February 2026

Your moment

Four months as CEO.
Four founders still here.
$21M just raised.

The Mecademic website still lists Jonathan Coulombe as CEO. That detail is not small. It is a signal of the transition that has not fully landed.

Harvard Business Review, 2026
2-3x

Founder-to-new-CEO transitions
fail at this rate vs. other transitions

The cause is almost never incompetence. It is ambiguity. Nobody is sure who is actually in charge.

The pattern

The risk is not conflict.
It is ambiguity.

What happens

Founder keeps making calls in their old domain. New CEO holds the title but not the full room. People build habits around the confusion.

Why it compounds

Every week without clarity, the ambiguity gets harder to resolve. People stop asking who decides. They just route around the question.

Move 1

Credit the past.
Claim the future.

"Jonathan and Ilian built the world's smallest, most precise industrial robot. My job is to make sure the world gets to use it."

This names their contribution without claiming it. Establishes your mandate without diminishing theirs. Frames the role as amplification, not replacement.

Move 2

State the lanes. Out loud.
In writing. Repeatedly.

Founders own

Engineering. Product. R&D. The precision that defines the brand.

CEO owns

Growth. Global presence. Governance. Commercial strategy.

Skygauge Robotics (Canadian, Dec 2025) used this framing: "planned succession that allows the founder to focus on what he does best." That language turns a power shift into a compliment.

Move 3

The next 100 hires
will never meet the founders.

The people joining in the US, Europe, and Asia-Pacific will know Mecademic through your version of the story. That version becomes the operating narrative whether you write it deliberately or not.

Three paragraphs. Where we came from. What changed. Where we are going. Ninety seconds. Repeatable across every context.

Write it this week.

Share it with the founders first. Get their feedback, not their approval. Then use it in every all-hands for six months.

The CEO who writes the story first is the one who leads.
The one who waits discovers, a year later, that three competing versions have taken root.
