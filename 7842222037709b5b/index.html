The Outsider CEO Playbook

Prepared for Philippe Beaulieu · February 21, 2026

You were promoted to CEO four months ago. Two days later, you announced a $21M raise and a global expansion across three continents. Four founders are still on the leadership team.

The organization is watching.

The Risk

Founder CEO transitions carry 2-3x the failure rate of other CEO transitions. — Harvard Business Review, January 2026

The risk is not conflict. It is ambiguity. Employees do not know who to listen to. The founder keeps making decisions in their old domain. The CEO holds the title but not the full authority.

This is not a personality problem. It is a communication architecture problem.

Three Moves

1. Honor the Origin, Own the Future

The language:

“Jonathan and Ilian built something remarkable. The world’s smallest, most precise industrial robot. My job is to make sure the world gets to use it.”

  • Names the founders’ contribution without claiming it
  • Establishes the CEO’s mandate without diminishing theirs
  • Frames the role as amplification, not replacement

The trap: over-crediting makes the CEO invisible. Under-crediting alienates the founding team.

2. Define the Lanes, Publicly

Founders Own CEO Owns
Engineering, product, R&D Growth, global presence
The precision that defines the brand Governance, commercial strategy

Visible is not the same as stated. Say it in every all-hands. Every board update. Every onboarding deck.

The moment someone has to guess who owns a decision, the ambiguity has already cost you.

3. Build the Narrative for the Next 100 Hires

The people joining in the US, Europe, and Asia-Pacific will never have met the founders in the Montreal lab. Their understanding of Mecademic comes from you.

You need a 90-second version that answers three questions:

  1. Where did we come from? The founders, the research, the precision obsession
  2. What changed? The market caught up, the company needed global reach
  3. Where are we going? Precision robotics as critical infrastructure across 40+ countries

The Window

You are at month four. The funding announcement creates attention. The next eight months of hiring across three continents are when the narrative either takes hold or fragments.

Skygauge Robotics (Canadian robotics, December 2025) went through the same dynamic. Their framing: “planned succession that strengthens executive structure, allowing the founder to focus on what he does best.”

Planned does not mean smooth. But it means deliberate.

One Thing to Do This Week

Write the “company narrative as told by the CEO” version. Three paragraphs. Share it with the founders first. Get their feedback, not their approval.

Then use it everywhere for six months.

The outsider CEO who waits for the narrative to develop organically discovers, twelve months later, that the organization has three competing stories about who it is. The one who writes it first, with the founders’ fingerprints on it, is the one who leads.
