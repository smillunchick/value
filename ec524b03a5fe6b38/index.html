One Story,
Three Rooms

Zero Homes is growing fast — $16.8M raised, five states, team doubling. The story that got you here needs to work differently in different rooms now.

Prepared for Grant Gunnison · February 2026

Your origin story is powerful

You grew up in your father's construction
business, went to MIT and NASA, then
came back to build for the trades.

That's a credibility bridge nobody else in the home electrification market can claim. But there's a pattern that shows up at your stage: the story that worked for the first $16M starts to fragment the moment you're telling it to three very different audiences at the same time.

Your lead investor Matt Eggers at Prelude described Zero Homes as "the missing digital infrastructure for home upgrades." That's the right framing for his room — but it's completely wrong for a contractor in Colorado who just wants to know if you'll fill his calendar.

Not product problems. Not market problems. The most common failure mode at your stage is telling the wrong version of the story to the wrong audience at the wrong time — and not realizing it because the transitions feel natural when you're the one in the room.

Three audiences, three languages

Investors

They hear platform infrastructure, TAM penetration, and unit economics. Your quote turnaround dropping from a full day to 30 minutes is the kind of friction metric that tells this story.

"The missing digital infrastructure for home upgrades" — Matt Eggers, Prelude

Contractors

They hear reliability, lead quality, and overhead gone. No fees, calendars filled, DOE/ACCA-certified sizing that handles the permitting headaches they're used to doing themselves.

"We do the part you hate so you can do the part you're great at"

Homeowners

They hear simplicity and trust. No in-home visit needed, money-back guarantee, 20% average savings. They want a heat pump installed without taking a day off work.

"You want to electrify. We make it easy."

The connective thread

Friction.

This is the single word that works in every room. For investors, friction reduction means unit economics. For contractors, it means overhead eliminated. For homeowners, it means simplicity. When you're unsure which version of the story to tell, return to friction. It anchors all three.

One word, three meanings

Investors

Friction reduction as platform efficiency — quote speed dropping from a day to 30 minutes, no truck rolls for quoting, a remote-first cost advantage that competitors with field teams can't match.

Contractors

Friction reduction as overhead elimination — no lead generation fees, automated system design and permitting, and a certified sizing process that removes the technical guesswork from their plate entirely.

Homeowners

Friction reduction as simplicity and trust — no in-home visit needed to get a quote, a money-back guarantee that removes the risk, and 20% average savings compared to going direct.

25 people → 50 people, across multiple states

The mission needs to carry
without the missionary.

Right now, the origin story comes through because you're in the room telling it. At 50 people across five states, you won't be in every room — and the new hires in Colorado or Texas will learn the company's purpose from whatever version reaches them first.

Write it down. Two paragraphs: your father's business, the overhead killing independent contractors, the decision to leave aerospace and come back. Put it in the onboarding deck — not as a poster on the wall, but as the answer to "why did we build it this way?"

The founders who get this transition right codify the culture before they need to. The ones who wait discover too late that the story has drifted into three different versions nobody agreed on.
