One Story, Three Rooms

Prepared for Grant Gunnison · February 21, 2026

I went down the rabbit hole on your interviews after seeing the Series A announcement, and the thing that stuck with me was not the technology. It was the fact that you grew up in your father’s construction business, went to MIT and NASA, and then came back to build software for the exact people your father worked alongside. That is a story nobody else in the home electrification market can tell.

But here is the thing I keep seeing with founders at your stage: the story that got you here starts to fragment the moment you try to tell it to three different audiences at the same time.

Your lead investor, Matt Eggers at Prelude, described Zero Homes as “the missing digital infrastructure for home upgrades.” That is the right framing for his room. But it is completely wrong for a contractor in Colorado who wants to know if you are going to fill his calendar or waste his time. And both of those are wrong for a homeowner who just wants a heat pump installed without taking a day off work.

You are already telling three different stories. You may not have noticed because the transitions feel natural when you are the one in the room. The problem comes when you are not in the room. When it is a sales rep in a new state, or a new hire explaining the company to a contractor, or a landing page trying to do the work of all three conversations at once.

McKinsey looked at 3,164 Series A companies and found that investors attribute 65 percent of portfolio failures to people and organizational issues, not product or market problems. The most common version of that failure is not building the wrong product. It is telling the wrong story to the wrong audience at the wrong time.

If there is one thing I would focus on, it is this: find the single word that works in every room. I think for Zero Homes it is friction.

For investors, friction reduction means platform efficiency and unit economics. Your quote turnaround moving from a day to thirty minutes is a friction metric.

For contractors, friction reduction means you handle the overhead they hate. “We do the part you hate so you can do the part you are great at.” That sentence, which you basically already use, is the whole contractor pitch.

For homeowners, friction reduction means simplicity. “You want to electrify. We make it easy.”

Same core idea. Three different expressions. The word “friction” is the anchor that keeps them coherent.

The other thing I would flag: you are about to double from 25 to 50 people across multiple states. Right now, the mission and the culture come through because you are in the room saying them. At 50 people, you will not be in every room. The origin story — your dad’s business, the overhead killing independent contractors, the decision to leave aerospace — needs to exist as an artifact, not just as something Grant tells over coffee.

Write it down. Two paragraphs. Put it in the onboarding deck. Not as a poster on the wall, but as the answer to “why did we build it this way?” The founders who get this transition right codify the culture before they need to. The ones who wait find out too late that the story has drifted.
