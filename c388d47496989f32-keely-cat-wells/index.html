Beyond the Backlash

Why disability inclusion is structurally different from the rest of DEI — and what that means for Making Space.

02
The Structural Difference

Disability inclusion has a legal floor others don't

The constitutional arguments used to dismantle race-conscious programs cannot be applied to disability. The ADA remains in force. Section 503 still requires federal contractors to take affirmative action on disability hiring. The 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause does not apply.

  • 8,800 ADA Title III federal lawsuits in 2024, up 7% from 2023
  • 37% surge in ADA website accessibility lawsuits in first half of 2025
  • Companies dismantling disability programs face rising legal exposure

Sources: Brown Goldstein Levy, ADA Title III, ADA National Network

03
Evidence
2.6x

more net income generated by companies leading on disability inclusion criteria, tracked across 346 companies from 2015 to 2022. Revenue was 1.6x higher, productivity 25% higher.

The cost objection collapses under data. 58% of workplace accommodations cost nothing. The rest average $500 one-time. Employers can access the Work Opportunity Tax Credit ($2,400 per hire) and Disabled Access Credit (up to $5,000/year).

Accenture 2023 · JAN · IRS WOTC

04
The Reframe

Companies that got the positioning right

JPMorgan Chase

Renamed diversity to DOI. Their BeST program for neurodivergent workers survived restructuring because it was never positioned as charity.

DXC Dandelion

Employs neurodivergent talent in cybersecurity, QA, and AI training. Positioned as a technical talent pipeline, not an inclusion initiative.

SAP Autism at Work

Frames autistic employees as competitive advantage in testing and data analysis. Coalition includes EY, Ford, HPE, Microsoft. Survived every round of cuts.

The pattern: when disability hiring is positioned as workforce innovation, it becomes immune to the political cycle.

05
Implications

What this means for Making Space

  • Enterprise procurement commitment to disability-owned businesses doubled from 27% to 56% between 2015 and 2024.
  • 542 corporations participated in the Disability Equality Index in 2024, including 71 Fortune 100 companies.
  • Disabled workforce participation has grown from 29% to 37% since 2018, driven by remote work and digital accessibility.
  • Companies cutting generic DEI are simultaneously facing rising ADA litigation, seeking talent pipelines, and searching for language that lets them invest without political backlash.

Sources: Disability:IN 2024, Accenture
