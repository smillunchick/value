Beyond the Backlash: Why Disability Inclusion Is Structurally Different

One in five companies have cut their DEI programs. But the data tells a more interesting story about where the money is actually going, and why disability-focused platforms are positioned to grow through the shift.

Prepared for Keely Cat-Wells · March 2026

The DEI retreat is real, but it is not total

The 2025 data shows a more segmented picture than the headlines suggest. About 20% of companies fully eliminated DEI programs after the political shift. But the majority did not. 65% held budgets steady. 23% actually increased spending. The companies that cut programs redirected 51% of the funds to general operations and 40% to AI and technology tools. The budget did not disappear. It migrated toward productivity framing.

Disability inclusion has a legal floor that race and gender DEI does not

The constitutional arguments used to dismantle race-conscious programs cannot be applied to disability. The ADA remains in force. Section 503 of the Rehabilitation Act still mandates affirmative action for federal contractors. And the enforcement environment is tightening, not loosening. Companies that dismantle disability programs face rising legal exposure. Companies that invest in them are building a compliance moat.

The financial case survives a CFO review

Accenture's longitudinal study tracked 346 companies over seven years through the Disability Equality Index. The companies leading on disability inclusion criteria consistently outperformed their peers across every financial metric. And the cost objection collapses under the data: more than half of all workplace accommodations cost nothing at all.

The companies that survived the backlash reframed early

Three patterns from organizations that separated disability hiring from the broader DEI conversation and positioned it as workforce strategy. Their programs were never on the chopping block because they were never framed as charity.

JPMorgan Chase — BeST Program

Renamed diversity to DOI (Diversity, Opportunity, Inclusion). The BeST program employing neurodivergent workers across seven US locations remained intact throughout restructuring. Disability was infrastructure, not an initiative.

DXC Technology — Dandelion Program

Positions neurodivergent employees in cybersecurity, QA, and AI training roles. Framed as a talent pipeline for technical skills, not inclusion. Won Disability:IN's Autism Inclusion Company of the Year 2024.

SAP — Autism at Work Coalition

Now includes EY, Ford, HPE, and Microsoft. Frames autistic employees as competitive workforce advantage in software testing and data analysis. Survived every round of corporate cost-cutting because it was never categorized as DEI spending.

Sources: JPMorgan Chase, DXC, Autism at Work

What this means for Making Space

The companies cutting generic DEI are simultaneously facing rising ADA litigation, searching for talent pipeline solutions, and looking for language that lets them invest in inclusion without triggering political backlash.

Enterprise procurement commitment to disability-owned businesses doubled from 27% to 56% between 2015 and 2024. 542 corporations participated in the Disability Equality Index last year, including 71 Fortune 100 companies. Disabled workforce participation has grown from 29% to 37% since 2018.

A platform that frames disability hiring as workforce innovation, backs it with compliance data, and delivers measurable placement outcomes is not vulnerable to the DEI cycle. It is the thing that replaces the DEI cycle. The question is not whether the market exists. It is whether Making Space claims the positioning before someone else builds it from the other direction.
