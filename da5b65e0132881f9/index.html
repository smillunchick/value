What translates perfectly

Three things from your enterprise
playbook that defense actually needs.

Customer obsession

Your 100-customers-in-100-days approach at Splunk works in defense. The military values leaders who listen before they build. The difference: in enterprise, customers buy quarterly. In defense, buying cycles are measured in years, and the customer who funds the program may not be the operator who uses the product.

Platform thinking

Hivemind as a licensable software layer across airframes is pure enterprise software strategy applied to defense hardware. This is genuinely differentiated. Nobody else in defense tech is thinking like a platform company — and that instinct is worth protecting.

Operational scaling

Getting from $300M toward $1B revenue requires the same discipline in defense as it does in enterprise: the right executive team, repeatable processes, and careful cash management. You've done this twice. The muscle memory is real.

The growth trajectory
3

defense companies in the last 20 years have hit $1B revenue on a startup trajectory

Palantir, Anduril, and SpaceX's government division. Shield AI's target is 70–100% annual growth to reach that mark by fiscal 2028. What those three had in common: a CEO who could speak both languages — software velocity and strategic deterrence.

What doesn't translate

Three places where enterprise
instincts will mislead you.

Credibility currency

In enterprise, credibility comes from revenue growth and customer logos. In defense, it comes from mission understanding and relationships with senior military leadership. The Hill, the Pentagon, and the combatant commands need to see someone who understands why autonomy matters for national security — not just how it works technically.

The ethics question

You told Fortune Shield AI "literally spends no time talking about the politics of particular missions." In enterprise software, that's fine. In defense, the autonomous weapons conversation is coming whether you engage or not. The CEOs who shape it proactively control their narrative. The ones who stay silent let critics write it for them.

The founder dynamic

In enterprise software, the hired CEO replaces the founder cleanly. In defense tech, the founder's vision is the company's identity. Ryan and Brandon Tseng built Shield AI's culture, military relationships, and technical vision. A tripartite leadership structure requires relational sophistication that no amount of operational excellence provides.

The narrative opportunity

Hivemind on the CCA is the proof point.
The story around it isn't there yet.

Shield AI's February 2026 CCA contract — Hivemind running on Anduril's YFQ-44A — is the kind of milestone that validates the entire platform strategy. But the announcement reads like a press release: "Shield AI is proud to be named a mission autonomy provider."

Compare that to how Palantir or Anduril would frame the same win — with a strategic vision about what autonomous combat aviation means for deterrence, for pilot safety, for the future of air superiority. The technology speaks for itself. What's missing is a narrative that connects it to a vision of what American defense should look like in 2030.

You have the operational credibility. The missing piece is a strategic voice that belongs to the CEO — not the comms team, not the co-founders. That narrative doesn't come from press releases. It comes from the person the board hired to take Shield AI from a defense startup to a defense institution.
