The Narrative Shift

Prepared for Rohit Agarwal · February 21, 2026

Your Series A announcement has one line that I keep coming back to: “Engineering gets reliability. Finance gets accountability. Leadership gets answers.”

You already know what is coming. The question is whether you have time to get ahead of it.

Portkey won 24,000 developer organizations by being the thing that never breaks. That is a real achievement and it is the reason Elevation Capital wrote the check. But the next phase of the company requires you to win rooms full of people who do not know what a token is and do not care.

A January 2026 survey of 266 CIOs and CTOs found that 46 percent of enterprise AI buying decisions now come from business leaders, not technical teams. Eighty-six percent of them track cost reduction as their primary metric. They are not evaluating AI gateways on latency benchmarks. They are evaluating on whether someone can tell them where the money is going.

This is the part that breaks for developer-tool companies moving upmarket. The product keeps working. The narrative stops working. The language that makes engineers trust you is the same language that makes a CFO’s eyes glaze over. Not because it is wrong, but because it answers a question they are not asking.

Here is a specific example. You process 500 billion tokens daily. That number is deeply meaningful to a VP of Engineering. It is a meaningless number to the person who approves the purchase order.

The same fact, reframed: “We manage an estimated $180 million in annualized AI spend and can show you exactly where every dollar goes across every model, every team, and every use case.”

Same truth. Completely different reaction. The first version says “we are big.” The second version says “your budget is visible.”

The compliance version is different again. “Every AI decision your organization makes, across every model and every team, is logged with a full audit trail and enforced against your policies in real time.” Eighty-four percent of enterprise CXOs say integrated security and compliance are mandatory before they will approve an AI tool. That is the room that can kill a deal the engineering team already loves.

There is also a competitive angle worth naming. TrueFoundry published a comparison in January that frames Portkey as “application-scoped, not organization-wide.” That characterization is not true today, but it could become true in the market’s perception if you do not define the category first. You said your priority is governance for agentic AI. Nobody owns that category yet. The company that defines what “AI governance platform” means gets to decide whether Portkey is a feature or the whole story.

None of this requires you to become a different person. The conviction that built something 24,000 organizations rely on is the same conviction that will land in enterprise rooms. The vocabulary just has to shift. You wrote “AI that never breaks.” That is not a developer tagline. That is a promise. The work is making sure the CFO and the CISO hear it as the same promise the engineering team does.
