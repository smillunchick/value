How “AI That Never Breaks” Lands in Three Enterprise Rooms

Prepared for Rohit Agarwal

Prepared by Pathos Labs · February 2026

You just raised $15M to expand go-to-market. Your Series A blog post includes one line that tells me you already sense what is coming: “Engineering gets reliability. Finance gets accountability. Leadership gets answers.”

That is the right instinct. It is also the beginning of a harder conversation, not the end of one. Because the story that won 24,000 developer organizations does not automatically translate into the story that wins a Fortune 500 CFO.

The Inflection Point

When a developer-tool company raises a Series A to move upmarket, the product often continues to work beautifully. What breaks is the narrative. The technical language that made engineers trust you is the same language that makes finance and compliance buyers hesitate. Not because it is wrong, but because it answers a question they are not asking.

Research from GoNarrative found that “the story that got you to Series A won’t get you to Series B. The message that fired up early adopters might confuse the enterprise customers you now need.” Seventy percent of startups fail the narrative test at this exact stage transition. [Confidence: High]

A January 2026 survey of 266 CIOs, CTOs, and CISOs by the Mayfield CXO Network found that 46% of enterprise AI buying decisions now come from business leaders, not technical teams. Eighty-six percent track cost reduction. Sixty-one percent track revenue growth. Sixty-six percent want defined KPIs before they sign. [Confidence: High]

The buyers are changing. The narrative needs to change with them.

The Three Rooms

Room 1: Engineering (The Room You Already Own)

What they hear: Latency. Uptime. Developer experience. Integration speed. Open-source community.

What Portkey already does well here: 99.9999% uptime, 60+ provider integrations, 6,200+ GitHub stars, 50 commits to the gateway per day. “AI that never breaks.”

The risk: Assuming the rest of the organization speaks this language. Your 500 billion tokens daily is deeply meaningful to a VP of Engineering. It is a meaningless number to the person who approves the purchase order.

Room 2: Finance (The Room That Writes the Check)

What they hear: Cost attribution. Budget predictability. Spend accountability. Vendor consolidation ROI.

The translation: “500 billion tokens processed daily” becomes “we manage an estimated $180 million in annualized AI spend and can show you exactly where every dollar goes across every model, every team, and every use case.”

Eighty-six percent of enterprise AI buyers now track cost reduction as a primary metric. The CFO does not care about tokens. The CFO cares about whether the AI budget is visible, attributable, and within policy. [Confidence: High]

Your Series A announcement already hints at this: “What are we spending? Is it within budget?” That is the right question to be asking on the CFO’s behalf. The deliverable here is a cost governance narrative, not a performance narrative.

Room 3: Security and Compliance (The Room That Can Kill the Deal)

What they hear: Audit trails. Data residency. Policy enforcement. SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA compliance. Access controls. Identity and permissions.

The translation: “AI guardrails” becomes “real-time policy enforcement with a full audit trail for every AI decision your organization makes, across every model and every team.”

Eighty-four percent of CXOs say integrated data security and compliance are mandatory before they will approve an AI tool. Sixty-five percent combine custom in-house systems with AI-native vendor tools. [Confidence: High]

This means the enterprise sale is not “replace what you have.” It is “govern what you have already built.” Portkey’s positioning as a unified control plane is strategically correct. The narrative needs to emphasize that Portkey does not replace the enterprise’s existing AI investments. It governs them.

The Competitive Window

A January 2026 competitive analysis from TrueFoundry explicitly positions Portkey as “application-scoped, not organization-wide” and “best for single-team LLM applications.” [Confidence: High]

This framing is dangerous not because it is true but because it could become true in the market’s perception if left unanswered. The narrative you let others write about you becomes the narrative you have to fight against. And fighting a narrative that has already taken hold is significantly harder than defining one before it solidifies.

Your stated priority of “governance for agentic AI” is a category that no one has fully defined yet. That is a window. Governance implies organization-wide scope. It implies compliance, audit, accountability. If Portkey defines what “AI governance platform” means before the category crystallizes around a competitor’s framing, the TrueFoundry characterization becomes irrelevant.

One Translation Exercise

Take a single Portkey metric and run it through all three rooms:

“120 million daily requests”

  • Engineering room: “Battle-tested at a scale that most AI infrastructure never reaches. 120 million requests daily, zero downtime.”
  • Finance room: “120 million AI decisions per day under active cost management. Every request tracked, attributed, and within budget policy.”
  • Compliance room: “120 million auditable AI decisions per day with full trace. Every request logged, every policy enforced in real time.”

Same fact. Three framings. Three different reasons to buy.

The Founder’s Role

Here is the pattern from companies that have successfully made this transition. Honeycomb, the observability platform, expanded its leadership team with a CMO and CRO specifically to accelerate enterprise growth, recognizing that the founder could not carry both the technical vision and the enterprise sales narrative alone. Recall.ai’s CEO David Gu spent 80% of his early engineering time on recording problems before pivoting to infrastructure and then systematically learning enterprise sales by recording and reviewing every pitch. [Confidence: High]

The lesson is not “become a different person.” The lesson is that the same conviction that built a product 24,000 organizations trust needs to be translated into the language of each room. The conviction stays. The vocabulary shifts.

You wrote “AI that never breaks.” That is not a developer tagline. That is a promise. The question is whether the CFO and the CISO hear it as the same promise the engineering team does. Right now, they cannot. The translation is the work.
