Trust Architecture

How AI platforms earn "system of record" status with patent professionals, and what that means for DeepIP's next chapter.

Prepared for FX Leduc · March 2026

Patent professionals are adopting AI faster than they trust it. That tension is the whole story.

01
The Paradox

Patent professionals are using AI they don't fully trust

67%

of legal professionals have had to override or correct AI-generated output

58%

would not submit AI-drafted work to a regulator or court without heavy review

Nearly seven in ten legal professionals now use generative AI for work. That number doubled in a single year. But most firms still lack formal AI policies or training programs, which means the adoption is outrunning the trust. Patent work amplifies this tension because every claim, every citation, every prior art reference carries legal and commercial consequences that compound over years. A hallucinated citation in a patent prosecution isn't just embarrassing. It can invalidate an entire filing, expose malpractice liability, and cost a client millions in lost IP protection.

Sources: Paragon Legal / Legal Cheek, Feb 2026 · 8am Legal Industry Report, Mar 2026

Five specific barriers explain why, and they're not the ones most AI companies think about.

02
The Trust Gap

Five barriers keep AI tools from becoming systems of record in legal

  • Expertise gap. 32% of law firms say they lack the internal knowledge to evaluate AI tools properly, let alone rely on them for cumulative work product. Most firms have no one whose job it is to vet these tools, so adoption happens informally and inconsistently.
  • Strategy vacuum. Only 22% of firms have a clear AI strategy. The rest are running disconnected pilots that generate signal but never reach firm-wide adoption. Without a strategy, each practice group picks its own tools, creating fragmentation that makes enterprise trust impossible.
  • Data dilemma. 41% cite data privacy as their primary concern. Patent data is especially sensitive: invention disclosures, competitive filings, prosecution history. Sending that data to a third-party model feels like handing a competitor your playbook.
  • Accuracy anxiety. High-profile cases of AI-generated fictitious citations created what firms call an "AI tax," the added burden of auditing every output before it goes anywhere official. In patent work, where a single missed reference can torpedo a prosecution, that tax is especially high.
  • Cultural caution. Legal professionals are trained to manage risk. What looks like resistance is usually hesitation. They want to move forward, but not without guardrails and clear accountability. The profession's entire incentive structure rewards thoroughness over speed.

Sources: Forge Technologies, Feb 2026 · DiscoverLex, Feb 2026

One company cracked this. Here's exactly how they did it.

03
What Worked

Harvey's playbook: three pillars that built $100M ARR in legal AI

Trust Stack

Custom legal models trained on all U.S. case law. A multi-layered verification system that decomposes responses into individual claims, cross-references each against authoritative sources, and flags inconsistencies before they reach users. Hallucination rate: 0.2%. That number matters because it's low enough to change behavior. Lawyers stopped double-checking everything and started trusting the output by default.

Beachhead Flywheel

Won Allen & Overy first, 4,000 lawyers across 43 jurisdictions. Targeted high-effort, low-risk use cases (first drafts, summaries). Positioned as assistant, not replacement. Success created FOMO that pulled the rest of the Am Law 100 inbound. The key insight: they didn't try to sell trust. They let one firm prove it, then let the market do the selling.

Ecosystem Moat

Integrated with LexisNexis for Shepard's citation validation, the industry's century-old standard. Embedded in Microsoft 365 so lawyers never leave their existing tools. Became ambient infrastructure, not a destination app. This is the move that made Harvey sticky: by the time a firm considers switching, Harvey is woven into every workflow.

The pattern: trust wasn't marketed. It was architected into every layer of the product, then proven through a single high-profile customer who made the risk feel manageable for everyone else.

But Harvey solved this for general practice law. Patent work is a different animal entirely.

The Deeper Pattern

The shift from "system of record" to "system of intelligence"

"For decades, enterprise value accrued to Systems of Record, rigid databases wrapped in workflow. Now it's shifting to Systems of Intelligence that layer reasoning on top of where truth already lives."
Activant Capital, "Systems of Intelligence," Jun 2025

This is the real strategic question for any AI platform in a conservative profession. A system of record earns its position by being the place where canonical truth lives, where decisions are traceable, where history is preserved, where accountability is clear. A system of intelligence earns its position by making the people who use it meaningfully better at their jobs. The platforms that win are the ones that do both, embedding intelligence into the record itself so professionals never have to choose between speed and traceability. In patent work, this convergence is especially powerful because a single patent family can span 15 years of prosecution history across multiple jurisdictions. Whoever holds that history holds the relationship.

This is where DeepIP's bet gets interesting.

05
What This Means for DeepIP

You're building in exactly the right gap

Most AI patent tools solve one step well: a better draft, a faster search, a cleaner office action response. DeepIP's bet is that patent work is cumulative. It spans years, teams, decisions. The platform that holds the full lifecycle holds the relationship. That's a fundamentally different product than a point solution, and it requires a fundamentally different kind of trust.

  • Embedding inside Word and existing IP management systems was the right instinct. Harvey's Microsoft 365 integration proved that ambient presence beats destination apps. DeepIP's reported 20% higher adoption and 40% more usage when embedded in existing tools confirms the pattern. The question now is whether that embedding goes deep enough to become the default workspace, not just a convenient sidebar.
  • The leap from 400 to 4,000 firms is where the trust architecture matters most. At 400, your sales team can personally vouch for every deployment. At 4,000, trust has to be structural, built into the product, the audit trail, the way the system explains its reasoning. This is the transition where most legal AI companies stall.
  • Having a registered patent attorney on the customer support team is the kind of detail that compounds. It signals that DeepIP respects the domain, not just the technology. That signal travels through a profession that talks to itself constantly.
  • The dual-market reality of serving both law firms and corporate IP teams means building trust with two different buyer psychology profiles at once. Law firms care about precision and defensibility. Corporate teams care about speed and portfolio visibility. The product has to speak both languages without diluting either one.

Which brings us to the question that matters most right now.
