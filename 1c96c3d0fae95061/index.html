The paradox

You helped build one of the best-run
companies in any industry.

Those are your own words about Intuitive Surgical, and they ring true. Fifteen years helping scale the company from $9 million to $1.8 billion in revenue earns you the right to say that without it sounding like flattery.

Now you're leading its most credible challenger. And every time you speak publicly, you're navigating a razor-thin line: too critical of Intuitive and you lose credibility with the surgical community that trusts them. Too deferential and you lose the narrative that makes Virtual Incision matter.

This is the central communications challenge of your CEO role, and it doesn't have a permanent solution. It has a practice.

The pattern is consistent across industries. But in surgical robotics it's especially pronounced: CMR Surgical, Medtronic Hugo, and Vicarious Surgical all struggle with the same narrative tension you face. The difference is your form factor. MIRA weighs two pounds. You aren't arguing about being a better version of the same thing. You're arguing about access.

Three audiences, three registers

The insurgent story needs to
sound different in every room.

Surgeons

Lead with clinical evidence and access

You already trust robotic surgery. MIRA brings it to the patients and facilities that can't access it today. Don't mention the incumbent by name. Let the form factor speak for itself.

Investors

Lead with TAM expansion, not share theft

The $7.5B surgical robotics market is growing, and MIRA opens segments—ASCs, rural hospitals, outpatient—that cart-based systems physically cannot serve. New market, not zero-sum game.

Talent

Lead with mission and legacy

Your coaching-tree philosophy is your recruiting edge. People who join Virtual Incision get to build the next era of surgical robotics, not maintain the current one. That distinction matters to the best engineers.

Selling the transformation, not the feature

You already know this principle.
You pioneered it.

Early in your career at Intuitive, you told the story of selling to Houston hospitals by aligning what the surgeon needed with what the administrator needed—two different pitches dovetailed into one decision. The feature was a robotic arm. The transformation was becoming the hospital that attracts the best surgeons in the region.

That same principle works in reverse now. Don't sell features—MIRA is smaller, cheaper, portable. Sell the transformation: you could be the hospital that brings robotic surgery to your community for the first time. The story that built da Vinci's dominance is now the story that expands the market beyond it.

The best part: you aren't learning someone else's framework. You're applying the one you already proved works—just from the other side of the table.

What the best insurgent CEOs do differently

Four moves that separate the credible
challengers from the bitter ex-employees.

1. Praise the incumbent explicitly and specifically. It builds credibility and signals you're not running on resentment. When Jensen Huang talks about Intel's manufacturing legacy, it makes his own chip story stronger, not weaker.

2. Redefine the category rather than competing within it. You're not in "surgical robotics." You're in "miniaturized robotics for surgical access." Different category, different metrics, different conversation entirely.

3. Own a different metric. Intuitive owns precision and clinical outcomes. You own access, portability, and affordability. Arguing superiority on their terms is a trap. Arguing on yours is a strategy.

4. Tell the origin story. Specifically, why someone who succeeded inside the incumbent chose to leave and build something different. That story, told honestly, is more persuasive than any product demo.
